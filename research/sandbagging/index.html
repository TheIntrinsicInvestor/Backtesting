01Study Design

What We Are Measuring, and the Paradox

Earnings sandbagging is the practice where management, through guidance and investor-relations channels, encourages analysts to lower their annual EPS estimates before the earnings release, making it easier to report a nominal beat. This study tests whether that pattern is detectable at scale across the S&P 500 using point-in-time (PIT) analyst estimate reconstruction.

We measure the walk-down as the percentage change in the mean consensus from T−270 (nine months before announcement) to T−2 (two days before). A negative value means the consensus fell over that window. Using IBES individual analyst estimates, we reconstruct the daily PIT consensus for each firm-year event between 2015 and 2025.

⚠
The paradox. Across all 6,131 included events, the median walk-down is +0.7%. The typical S&P 500 firm does not walk estimates down at all, it walks them up. The walk-down is not a universal phenomenon. It is selective, deployed specifically on the firms that would otherwise miss.

Classification

Each event is classified into one of three groups based on the relationship between the actual EPS, the original consensus, and the final consensus:

Genuine Beat (4,009 events)
Actual EPS exceeded the original consensus. The firm would have beaten regardless of any walk-down.
Manufactured Beat (849 events)
Actual EPS missed the original consensus but beat the final (walked-down) consensus. The beat was manufactured by walking the bar down.
Miss (1,273 events)
Actual EPS fell below even the walked-down final consensus.

Quality filters exclude events with non-positive consensus, sign flips between original and final, fewer than 3 active analysts at either snapshot, or an absolute original consensus below $0.10. Of 6,552 raw event-year combinations, 6,131 pass all filters and form the study universe.

02The Selective Walk-Down

The Walk-Down Is Deployed Selectively

The chart below shows the distribution of walk-down percentages across all included events. The overall distribution skews positive (more walk-ups than walk-downs), but the manufactured beats (red) are concentrated entirely in the flat and down bins. This is the core finding: the walk-down is not a market-wide phenomenon but a targeted tool used on specific firms.

The Hero Chart: Two Cohort Walk Curves

For each included event, the daily PIT consensus is normalised to its T−270 base (= 100). The chart below averages this normalised curve across the manufactured and genuine cohorts. The manufactured cohort (red) walks steadily down over nine months while the genuine cohort (green) drifts upward, reflecting natural upward earnings revisions for firms on track to beat. The Cross-Tab Clincher

If sandbagging were random or universal, we would expect manufactured beats to appear across all walk-down bins. The cross-tab below shows they do not. Of 849 manufactured beats, 709 came from firms that walked estimates down, 140 from firms that held estimates flat, and 0 came from firms that walked estimates up. Zero manufactured beats emerge from the up-walking bin, which confirms that the manufactured beat is definitionally tied to the walk-down.

03Who Gets Walked Down

Sandbagging Happens at the Most-Covered Names

A naive prior might expect sandbagging to occur at smaller, less-scrutinised firms where management has more influence over the analyst consensus. The data shows the opposite. Manufactured beats are concentrated at well-covered, large-cap names where the analyst community is densest and the incentive to produce a clean beat is highest.

The manufactured cohort has a median of 20 active analysts versus 20 for genuine beats. High analyst coverage means the consensus is harder to move, which makes the selective walk-down more conspicuous: management must guide many analysts to trim their estimates, not just one. That 56% of manufactured beats were within 5% of beating the original consensus underscores that the walk-down is often just enough, not a wholesale reset.

04Does It Pay?

Announcement Reaction and Post-Print Drift

If manufactured beats are qualitatively different from genuine beats, market prices should reflect that. We test two windows: (1) the announcement reaction (CAR T−1 to T+1) to see whether the market immediately discounts the manufactured quality, and (2) post-print drift (CAR T+1 to T+60) to test whether recognition is delayed. All returns are market-adjusted vs SPY over identical calendar windows. A long-genuine/short-manufactured strategy is evaluated on both.

Returns are price-only (dividends excluded). Events where the T+60 window extends beyond 2025-12-31 are censored and excluded from drift statistics (70 events removed). See Methodology for details.

What These Results Mean

The market initially over-celebrates manufactured beats: the announcement reaction is not muted but slightly elevated (+0.81% vs +0.59% for genuine beats). Recognition is delayed, not immediate. Over the subsequent 59 trading days, manufactured beats reverse (-1.49%) while genuine beats hold positive ground (+0.15%), producing a drift L/S spread of +1.65% (annual Sharpe 0.39 over 11 years). Any viable strategy must enter after the announcement, not before.

⚠
Conditional signal: practical execution is the barrier. The +1.65 pp 60-day drift spread (Sharpe 0.39 over 11 years) is statistically present. Three practical hurdles likely erode it in live trading: (1) shorting manufactured beats requires locating borrow on large-cap names around earnings, often at elevated fees; (2) the strategy competes with post-earnings drift traders already positioned in these names; (3) running ~849 short and ~4009 long positions per year generates meaningful transaction costs over a 59-trading-day holding period. A paper-trading exercise is warranted before committing capital.

05Methodology

Data and Construction Details

Key parameters and data choices are summarised below. The PIT reconstruction uses individual analyst estimates from IBES Detail rather than the pre-aggregated consensus, which avoids look-ahead bias from revision-timing differences in the summary table.

06Conclusions

Four Key Findings

✓
The walk-down is real, but selective. 17.5% of annual S&P 500 beats between 2015 and 2025 are manufactured by walking analyst estimates down over the nine months before announcement. The median S&P 500 firm does not walk estimates down. The phenomenon is selective, concentrated at firms that would otherwise miss.
✗
Zero manufactured beats from up-walking firms. Every one of the 849 manufactured beats comes from a firm in the down or flat walk-down bin. Not one manufactured beat occurs at a firm where the consensus rose. This is a structural feature of the classification, not a coincidence.
⚠
Sandbagging concentrates at the most-covered names. The manufactured cohort has a median of 20 active analysts vs 20 for genuine beats. Large-cap, heavily-covered names are the most active sandbagging sites, consistent with the hypothesis that the incentive to produce a clean institutional beat is highest where the audience is largest.
ⓘ
Statistical limits. The study uses price-only returns (dividends excluded), which slightly understates long-run performance for high-dividend names. The T+60 drift window censors 70 events where CRSP data ends before the exit date. Annual Sharpe estimates use 11 year-observations and should be treated as indicative only. All t-statistics are one-sample tests against zero with no multiple-comparison correction.
